Step 1 of 5 — Read & absorb

Photosynthesis — The Full Picture

Photosynthesis is how plants turn sunlight into sugar. It happens in two linked stages inside the chloroplast. First, the light-dependent reactions capture light energy in the thylakoid membrane — splitting water, producing ATP and NADPH, releasing oxygen. Then, the Calvin cycle uses that energy in the stroma to pull CO₂ from the air and build glucose.
Light reactions: thylakoid membrane → ATP, NADPH, O₂ released
Calvin cycle: stroma → CO₂ fixed into G3P using ATP + NADPH
Net input: CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose + O₂
Key Terms — lock these in
Chlorophyll Pigment that absorbs light energy
Thylakoid Membrane discs — site of light reactions
Stroma Fluid around thylakoids — Calvin cycle here
ATP Energy molecule, made in light reactions
NADPH Electron carrier from light reactions
G3P First sugar — product of Calvin cycle

Real exam-style questions

These are the kinds of questions that actually come up. Think it through, then reveal the model answer.

01
A plant is placed in a sealed container with a CO₂ sensor. After 2 hours in sunlight, CO₂ levels drop sharply. Explain why, using your knowledge of photosynthesis.
Application · 4 marks
The plant absorbs CO₂ for use in the Calvin cycle. During light reactions, ATP and NADPH are produced. These drive carbon fixation in the stroma, pulling CO₂ from the container to build G3P. Since the container is sealed, CO₂ is not replenished, so levels drop continuously while light is available.
02
Explain why a variegated leaf (green and white patches) only shows starch in the green areas after being exposed to light.
Analysis · 5 marks
White areas lack chlorophyll — the light-absorbing pigment needed to initiate photosynthesis. Without chlorophyll, no ATP or NADPH is produced, so the Calvin cycle cannot run and no glucose (or starch) is synthesised. Green areas contain chlorophyll, can carry out both stages, and therefore produce and store starch.
